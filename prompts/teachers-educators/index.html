15 ChatGPT Prompts for Teachers That Save Hours of Prep Time

Teachers spend more time on administrative and prep tasks than on actual teaching. These prompts help you build better lesson plans, write clearer feedback, and communicate with parents — in a fraction of the time.

Prompt 1 of 15
Lesson plan (any subject)
Write a detailed lesson plan for a [GRADE LEVEL] [SUBJECT] class on the topic '[TOPIC]'. Duration: [LENGTH] minutes. Include: learning objectives (3, using Bloom's taxonomy verbs), materials needed, warm-up activity (5 min), main instruction (with teacher notes), guided practice, independent practice, closure/exit ticket, and differentiation notes for advanced and struggling students.
💡 Pro tip: Using Bloom's taxonomy verbs (analyze, evaluate, create vs. 'learn about') produces lessons with measurable outcomes.
Prompt 2 of 15
Rubric generator
Create a [4/5]-point rubric for evaluating [ASSIGNMENT TYPE — e.g., persuasive essay, science lab report, group presentation] at the [GRADE LEVEL] level. Include [4-5] criteria. For each criterion, describe performance at every level from Excellent to Needs Improvement. Clear, student-friendly language.
💡 Pro tip: Sharing rubrics with students before the assignment reduces questions by 70% and improves quality.
Prompt 3 of 15
Parent email (academic concern)
Write an email to the parent of [STUDENT NAME — use placeholder] about a concern with their [ACADEMIC PERFORMANCE / BEHAVIOR / PARTICIPATION] in [SUBJECT]. Be specific but constructive. Avoid labels. Suggest a meeting. Offer 2-3 next steps that parent and teacher can take together. Under 200 words. Warm, solution-focused.
💡 Pro tip: The most effective parent emails describe specific observable behaviors, not personality traits.
Prompt 4 of 15
Student feedback (written work)
Write constructive feedback for a student's [ESSAY/PROJECT/ASSIGNMENT] on [TOPIC] at the [GRADE LEVEL] level. The work was [BRIEF DESCRIPTION OF QUALITY]. Provide: 2 specific strengths (with examples), 2 areas for improvement (with specific suggestions), and 1 encouragement. Under 150 words. Growth mindset language.
💡 Pro tip: Feedback framed as 'here's how to improve' rather than 'here's what's wrong' increases revision effort.
Prompt 5 of 15
Differentiated activity (3 levels)
Create a differentiated version of this activity: [ORIGINAL ACTIVITY DESCRIPTION] for a [GRADE LEVEL] [SUBJECT] class. Write 3 versions: (1) Scaffolded (for students below grade level), (2) On-grade, (3) Extension (for advanced students). Maintain the same learning objective across all 3. Each version: under 100 words of instructions.
💡 Pro tip: One activity, three levels. This strategy reduces prep time compared to building entirely separate tasks.
Prompt 6 of 15
Exit ticket (formative assessment)
Write 3 exit ticket prompts for a lesson on [TOPIC] for [GRADE LEVEL] students. Each prompt should assess a different level of understanding: (1) recall, (2) application, (3) reflection. Under 20 words each. Easy to write, easy to grade quickly.
💡 Pro tip: Exit tickets take 3 minutes to complete and give you the clearest picture of what students actually learned.
Prompt 7 of 15
Discussion question set (Socratic)
Write 8 Socratic discussion questions for [GRADE LEVEL] students about [TEXT/TOPIC/CONCEPT]. Include: 2 literal comprehension questions, 3 inferential questions, 2 evaluative questions, and 1 synthesis question. Order them from accessible to challenging. Each under 25 words.
💡 Pro tip: Start with literal questions so every student can participate. Build to the harder questions once discussion momentum is established.
Prompt 8 of 15
Newsletter (classroom update for parents)
Write a monthly classroom newsletter for parents of [GRADE LEVEL] students in [SUBJECT/CLASS]. Include: what we learned this month (2-3 highlights), upcoming topics/events, a tip for supporting learning at home, and any announcements. Friendly, brief, informative. Under 300 words. With a subject line.
💡 Pro tip: Monthly newsletters reduce the number of individual parent inquiries significantly.
Prompt 9 of 15
Accommodation plan note (IEP/504 alignment)
Write a short note documenting how I am implementing accommodations for a student with [ACCOMMODATION TYPE — e.g., extended time, preferential seating, visual supports] in my [SUBJECT] class. Include: what the accommodation is, how I apply it in the classroom, and evidence of impact. Under 150 words. For file documentation purposes.
💡 Pro tip: Brief, consistent documentation protects you professionally and demonstrates you're meeting legal obligations.
Prompt 10 of 15
Substitute teacher lesson plan
Write a substitute teacher lesson plan for a [GRADE LEVEL] [SUBJECT] class on [DATE]. Include: class schedule and transitions, attendance procedure, the main activity (self-contained, minimal explanation needed), behavior expectations, what to do if students finish early, and who to contact if there's a problem. Sub-friendly language.
💡 Pro tip: A good sub plan leaves nothing to interpretation — write it for someone who has never met your students.
Prompt 11 of 15
Recommendation letter (student)
Write a recommendation letter for student [NAME] applying to [PROGRAM/SCHOOL/SCHOLARSHIP]. Include: how long and in what context I know the student, 2 specific examples of their character and ability (with anecdotes), their strongest academic quality, one area of growth that shows self-awareness, and a strong closing endorsement. Under 400 words. Formal academic tone.
💡 Pro tip: Specific anecdotes (a moment in class, a project they led) make recommendation letters stand out from generic ones.
Prompt 12 of 15
Project-based learning brief (PBL)
Design a project-based learning brief for [GRADE LEVEL] students on the topic '[TOPIC]'. Include: the driving question, real-world context, subject connections ([SUBJECTS — e.g., math + science + writing]), student roles, deliverables, timeline (in phases), and assessment criteria. Engaging, authentic, student-centered.
💡 Pro tip: A strong driving question ('How might we...') creates intrinsic motivation that worksheet assignments can't match.
Prompt 13 of 15
Course syllabus section (expectations)
Write the classroom expectations section of a course syllabus for [GRADE LEVEL] [SUBJECT]. Cover: participation expectations, homework policy, late work policy, academic integrity, how grades are calculated, and how to get help. Student-friendly language. Include a 'course contract' statement for students to sign. Under 350 words.
💡 Pro tip: A clearly written syllabus reduces grade disputes and parent complaints dramatically.
Prompt 14 of 15
Student of the week announcement
Write a warm, encouraging 'student of the week' blurb for [STUDENT NAME (placeholder)] in [GRADE LEVEL]. Highlight: one academic strength, one character trait, and one personal interest the class might enjoy knowing. Under 75 words. Celebratory, inclusive, age-appropriate.
💡 Pro tip: Public recognition of non-academic strengths (kindness, effort, creativity) builds class culture.
Prompt 15 of 15
Professional development reflection (post-PD)
Write a 150-word reflection after attending a professional development session on '[PD TOPIC]'. Cover: one key insight you gained, how you plan to apply it in your classroom, and one question you still have. For a portfolio or required PD documentation log.
💡 Pro tip: Brief written reflections after PD sessions help you retain and apply what you learned 3x better.
