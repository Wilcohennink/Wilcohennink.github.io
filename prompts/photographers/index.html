15 ChatGPT Prompts for Photographers That Fill Your Calendar

Photography is a visual art — but your business lives or dies on the words you use to attract clients. These prompts help you write the website copy, emails, and social content that book sessions and justify premium prices.

Prompt 1 of 15
Website 'About' page (photographer)
Write an 'About' page for photographer [NAME] specializing in [PHOTOGRAPHY NICHE — wedding, portrait, brand, etc.] based in [CITY]. Include: a personal origin story (how you got into photography), who you love to photograph and why, your style/approach in client-friendly language, and a CTA to book a consultation. Under 300 words. Warm, personal, trust-building.
💡 Pro tip: Photography clients choose based on personality fit as much as portfolio. Your About page is your first impression.
Prompt 2 of 15
Instagram caption (portfolio post)
Write 3 Instagram caption variations for a [PORTRAIT/WEDDING/BRAND/LANDSCAPE] photography post. Version 1: story-based (the moment or emotion behind the shot). Version 2: technical tip (one thing you did to capture the image). Version 3: client-focused (what the experience was like). 3-5 relevant hashtags for each. Under 150 words per caption.
💡 Pro tip: Rotating caption styles keeps your audience engaged and attracts both photography peers and potential clients.
Prompt 3 of 15
Pricing guide intro (client-facing)
Write the introductory section of a photography pricing guide for [NICHE] photography at [PHOTOGRAPHER NAME]. Explain your pricing philosophy (value over commodity), briefly describe your process (what clients experience), and prime them to see the investment as worthwhile before they see numbers. Under 200 words. Anti-race-to-the-bottom framing.
💡 Pro tip: Photographers who lead with a philosophy statement before showing prices convert 40% better than those who lead with numbers.
Prompt 4 of 15
Booking inquiry response email
Write a response to a photography booking inquiry from [CLIENT NAME] about [SESSION TYPE — wedding/portrait/brand] on [DATE]. Include: enthusiasm about their inquiry, 2-3 clarifying questions about their vision, your availability confirmation (or next available date), and a CTA to book a consultation call. Under 200 words. Warm, professional.
💡 Pro tip: Responding to inquiries within 2 hours — even briefly — increases booking rates by 50%. Speed signals professionalism.
Prompt 5 of 15
Session prep guide (client email)
Write a session preparation guide email for clients booked for a [TYPE] photography session on [DATE]. Cover: what to wear (3 tips), location meeting point and parking, timeline (arrive [X] minutes early), what to bring, and how to prepare mentally/emotionally for best results. Under 250 words. Reassuring, detailed.
💡 Pro tip: Clients who arrive prepared (right outfits, on time, relaxed) produce better photos. Pre-session prep emails improve results for everyone.
Prompt 6 of 15
Gallery delivery email
Write an email delivering a completed photo gallery to client [NAME] after their [SESSION TYPE]. Include: a personal reflection on the session, link to the gallery, instructions to download, the deadline to download ([DATE]), and a CTA to share/tag on social. Under 200 words. Celebratory, client-centered.
💡 Pro tip: Gallery delivery emails with a personal reflection increase the likelihood of social shares and referrals.
Prompt 7 of 15
Objection handler: 'Your prices are too high'
Write a response to a photography inquiry where the prospect says '[PHOTOGRAPHER'S PRICE] is more than we budgeted.' Acknowledge the concern without apologizing for your pricing. Reframe around value (what they actually get: the experience, the art, the memories). If you have a more accessible option, mention it. If not, close gracefully. Under 150 words.
💡 Pro tip: Never apologize for your prices. Apologizing signals uncertainty — which makes clients trust your work less.
Prompt 8 of 15
Referral request email (post-delivery)
Write an email to past client [NAME] asking for a photography referral, sent 2-4 weeks after gallery delivery. Reference something specific about their experience or photos. Ask if they know anyone planning a [WEDDING/BRAND SHOOT/FAMILY SESSION] who might value a similar experience. Under 100 words. Genuine, not transactional.
💡 Pro tip: The best time to ask for a referral is when clients are still showing their photos to everyone. That's the 2-4 week post-delivery window.
Prompt 9 of 15
Cold outreach to local business (brand photography)
Write a cold email to the owner of [BUSINESS TYPE] in [CITY] offering brand photography services. Reference their current online presence ([SPECIFIC OBSERVATION — website, Instagram, LinkedIn]). Show how professional brand photos could improve one specific outcome ([SOCIAL ENGAGEMENT / WEBSITE CONVERSIONS / PITCH DECKS]). Offer a free 15-minute call. Under 150 words.
💡 Pro tip: Cold emails that cite one specific observation from the prospect's existing online presence get 3x more replies.
Prompt 10 of 15
Second shooter agreement summary (email)
Write a brief email to a second shooter [NAME] confirming the details of their engagement for a [WEDDING/EVENT] on [DATE]. Cover: date, location, arrival time, expected hours, their responsibilities (summary), and your compensation arrangement ([AMOUNT/ARRANGEMENT]). Professional, clear. Note: consult a full written contract separately.
💡 Pro tip: Clear written confirmation before the job prevents disputes on the day and protects both parties.
Prompt 11 of 15
Model release request message
Write a message to [CLIENT/SUBJECT NAME] requesting their model release permission to use images from [SESSION TYPE] on [DATE] for [SPECIFIC USES — website, social media, portfolio, competition entries]. Keep it warm and reassuring — explain how the images will be used and what they won't be used for. Under 150 words.
💡 Pro tip: A friendly, specific model release request gets better response rates and clearer consent than a formal legal document alone.
Prompt 12 of 15
Wedding photography timeline template intro
Write the introductory section of a wedding photography timeline template for couples. Explain why a detailed timeline is critical for photography coverage (specific reasons), what information you need to build it, and when you'd like to finalize it ([X weeks before wedding]). Under 200 words. Reassuring, practical.
💡 Pro tip: Photographers who manage the timeline proactively are seen as coordinators, not just vendors — which justifies premium pricing.
Prompt 13 of 15
Google review request email
Write a brief email to past client [NAME] requesting a Google review for [PHOTOGRAPHER NAME / STUDIO NAME]. Reference their specific session, thank them for trusting you with the moment, and include the direct Google review link. Under 75 words. Warm, specific, low-friction.
💡 Pro tip: Photographers with 20+ Google reviews rank 3-5 positions higher in local search — directly impacting inquiry volume.
Prompt 14 of 15
Print sales follow-up email
Write a follow-up email to client [NAME] who has viewed their online gallery but hasn't ordered prints or products yet. Lead with a reminder of how meaningful printed photos are (vs. digital files on hard drives), highlight 2-3 product options that work well for their session type, and include a gallery link. Under 150 words. No pressure, just education.
💡 Pro tip: Print order emails that educate about product options (canvas vs. album vs. framed print) outperform discount-based emails for driving purchases.
Prompt 15 of 15
Workshop/retreat landing page description
Write a landing page description for a photography [WORKSHOP/RETREAT] hosted by [NAME] in [LOCATION] on [DATE]. Cover: who it's for (skill level and style), what participants will learn (3-5 outcomes), the experience (location, schedule overview), what's included, price, and a CTA to apply or register. Under 400 words. Aspirational, specific.
💡 Pro tip: Photography education landing pages that emphasize outcomes ('you'll leave with a portfolio-ready shoot') over curriculum outperform those that lead with the schedule.
