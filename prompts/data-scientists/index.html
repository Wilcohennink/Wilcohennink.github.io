15 ChatGPT Prompts for Data Scientists That Translate Analysis into Impact

Data science impact is limited by communication. The best analysis in the world goes nowhere if stakeholders don't understand it. These prompts help you write clear findings, executive summaries, and content that builds your data science reputation.

Prompt 1 of 15
Executive summary (model or analysis results)
Write a non-technical executive summary of these analysis results: [DESCRIBE FINDINGS]. Audience: [C-SUITE/BUSINESS TEAM]. Cover: what question we were answering, what we found (3 key findings in plain language), business implications, and recommended action. No statistical jargon. Under 250 words.
💡 Pro tip: Every executive summary should answer: 'So what, and what do we do about it?' If it doesn't, it won't get read.
Prompt 2 of 15
Stakeholder presentation narrative (slide-by-slide)
Write a slide-by-slide narrative for a 10-slide data presentation on [PROJECT/ANALYSIS TOPIC]. For each slide, write: the slide title, 2-3 sentences of speaker notes, and the key message. Slides: (1) Problem, (2) Data overview, (3-7) Key findings, (8) Limitations, (9) Recommendations, (10) Next steps. Non-technical audience.
💡 Pro tip: Each slide should have one main message. If you can't state it in one sentence, the slide has too much on it.
Prompt 3 of 15
LinkedIn post (data insight for your niche)
Write a LinkedIn post sharing one interesting data insight from [FIELD/INDUSTRY] that would surprise most people. Structure: hook (the counterintuitive finding), 2-3 paragraphs explaining what the data shows and why it matters, and a 'what this means for [AUDIENCE]' takeaway. Under 1,200 characters. Cite your source.
💡 Pro tip: Data science professionals who share surprising findings on LinkedIn build authority faster than those who only post about their projects.
Prompt 4 of 15
Project README (technical documentation)
Write a README.md for a [PROJECT TYPE — ML model, data pipeline, analysis notebook] called [PROJECT NAME]. Include: project overview (2-3 sentences), problem statement, data sources, methodology/approach (4-5 steps), key findings or performance metrics, how to run/reproduce the project (step-by-step), dependencies, and contact info. Technical audience.
💡 Pro tip: A good README is what separates a portfolio project from a GitHub graveyard. Write it for the version of yourself 6 months from now.
Prompt 5 of 15
Model explanation for non-technical audience
Write a plain-English explanation of how a [MODEL TYPE — e.g., random forest, LSTM, clustering model] works, for a non-technical business audience. Use an analogy. Explain: what it does (input → output), why we chose it over alternatives, its main limitation, and how confident we should be in its outputs. Under 250 words. No equations.
💡 Pro tip: Analogies make models trustworthy to non-technical stakeholders. Trustworthy models get their recommendations acted on.
Prompt 6 of 15
Data quality issue memo
Write a memo to [TEAM/STAKEHOLDER] explaining a data quality issue found in [DATASET/SYSTEM]. Cover: what the issue is (plain language), when it was discovered, estimated impact (% of records affected, downstream impact on [ANALYSIS/MODELS]), root cause hypothesis, proposed fix, and recommended action while the fix is in progress. Under 250 words.
💡 Pro tip: Data quality memos that estimate business impact (not just technical impact) get prioritized for engineering fixes faster.
Prompt 7 of 15
Job interview prep (behavioral questions)
Write detailed answers to these 3 data science behavioral interview questions for [YOUR BACKGROUND]. Questions: (1) Tell me about a time your analysis led to a significant business decision. (2) Describe a situation where your model didn't work as expected — what did you do? (3) How have you explained a complex model to a non-technical stakeholder? Use the STAR format for each. Under 250 words per answer.
💡 Pro tip: Behavioral answers that include specific metrics ('the model reduced churn by 12%') are rated significantly higher by interviewers.
Prompt 8 of 15
Blog post / article (data science topic)
Write a 500-word blog post on [DATA SCIENCE TOPIC — e.g., common mistakes in A/B testing, feature engineering tips, how to choose evaluation metrics]. Audience: junior-to-mid data scientists. Structure: compelling hook, the core concept explained with an example, 3 practical takeaways, and a conclusion. Publish on Medium or personal blog.
💡 Pro tip: Data science blog posts with working code examples or specific numerical examples get 10x more shares than conceptual posts.
Prompt 9 of 15
Technical interview study guide
Create a study guide for a [DATA SCIENCE / ML ENGINEER / ANALYST] technical interview at a [COMPANY TYPE — startup, FAANG, finance]. Cover: top 10 concepts to review, 5 coding problems to practice (describe, don't write the code), 3 case study types to prepare for, and 3 questions to ask the interviewer. Organized by priority.
💡 Pro tip: Study guides organized by priority (what's most likely to come up) are more useful than exhaustive topic lists.
Prompt 10 of 15
Data ethics statement (for a project)
Write a data ethics statement for a [PROJECT TYPE — predictive model, recommender system, customer segmentation]. Cover: what data is used and who it describes, potential harms or biases in the model, steps taken to mitigate bias (3-4 specific measures), intended and unintended uses, and how the model will be monitored after deployment. Under 300 words.
💡 Pro tip: Proactive data ethics statements in project docs signal maturity and reduce legal/compliance escalations later.
Prompt 11 of 15
A/B test results summary (for product team)
Write an A/B test results summary for test [NAME/ID]. Test details: [VARIANT DESCRIPTION], [SAMPLE SIZE], [DURATION], [PRIMARY METRIC]. Results: [STATISTICAL SIGNIFICANCE, LIFT %]. Include: test objective, methodology, key results (primary + secondary metrics), interpretation, recommendation (ship/don't ship/iterate), and caveats. For a product and business audience. Under 300 words.
💡 Pro tip: A/B test summaries that include a clear 'ship / don't ship / iterate' recommendation are actioned 5x faster than those that leave it to interpretation.
Prompt 12 of 15
Data team newsletter (monthly)
Write a monthly data team newsletter section covering: one recent analysis or model shipped (with 1-line impact), one interesting data finding from the business, one data quality issue resolved, and one tool or technique the team learned this month. Under 250 words. For internal distribution to all teams.
💡 Pro tip: Data team newsletters build cross-functional visibility — teams who know what you're working on are more likely to involve you in decisions early.
Prompt 13 of 15
Metrics definition document
Write a metrics definition document for [METRIC NAME — e.g., 'active user', 'conversion rate', 'churn']. Cover: precise definition (what counts and what doesn't), calculation formula, data source, update frequency, known limitations or edge cases, and the team responsible for ownership. Under 200 words per metric. Template for up to 3 metrics.
💡 Pro tip: Metrics definition documents prevent the single most common source of data disagreements: everyone measuring the same thing differently.
Prompt 14 of 15
Portfolio project description (job search)
Write a portfolio project description for [PROJECT NAME] for a data scientist's resume or portfolio website. Cover: problem statement, data used, methodology (3-4 steps), key result (with metrics), tools used (Python, SQL, etc.), and a link to the code (placeholder). Under 150 words. Impact-focused.
💡 Pro tip: Portfolio descriptions that lead with business impact ('reduced customer churn by 15%') beat those that lead with technical methods every time.
Prompt 15 of 15
Cross-functional collaboration email (with engineering)
Write an email to the engineering team at [COMPANY] requesting their support for [DATA INFRASTRUCTURE NEED — e.g., new data pipeline, feature in data warehouse, logging event]. Include: what you need, why it matters (business context), estimated effort (your guess), and your proposed timeline. Under 200 words. Collaborative framing — they're partners, not a service desk.
💡 Pro tip: Framing engineering requests in terms of business outcomes (not just your analysis needs) gets them prioritized faster.
