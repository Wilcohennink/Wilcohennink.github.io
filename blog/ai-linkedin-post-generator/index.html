The 5 LinkedIn Post Formats That Get the Most Reach

1. The Contrarian Take

Opens with a statement that challenges conventional wisdom. Gets engagement through disagreement and curiosity.

Structure:

  • Hook: "Everyone says [X]. They're wrong."
  • Expand: 3–4 lines explaining your alternative view
  • Evidence: One specific example or data point
  • CTA: "What do you think?"

Example hook: "Everyone says you need a large audience to get leads from LinkedIn. You don't."

2. The Numbered List

High-value, scannable, easy to share. These posts tend to perform well because they're easy to skim and save.

Structure:

  • Hook: "X [things/lessons/mistakes] that [outcome]"
  • List items: 5–10 short, specific points — one per line
  • Close: One personal observation or takeaway
  • CTA: "Which one surprised you most?"

Example hook: "7 things I wish I knew before growing a LinkedIn audience from 0 to 20,000:"

3. The Personal Story

Vulnerability and authenticity drive the highest engagement on LinkedIn. Stories about failure, hard decisions, or turning points outperform most other formats.

Structure:

  • Hook: Start in the middle of the action (not "I want to share a story...")
  • Setup: What was the situation?
  • Tension: What went wrong or what decision had to be made?
  • Resolution: What happened?
  • Lesson: One clear takeaway
  • CTA: "Has this happened to you?"

Example hook: "Two years ago I was $40,000 in debt with a business that wasn't working. Here's what I changed."

4. The How-To / Tutorial

Educational content that saves someone time. Works best when it's specific to a niche challenge.

Structure:

  • Hook: "How to [do specific thing] in [timeframe or without X]"
  • Step-by-step: 5–8 short steps
  • Pro tip: One non-obvious insight
  • CTA: "Save this post for later" or "Which step trips you up most?"

Example hook: "How to get your first 10 paying clients without spending on ads (exactly what I did):"

5. The Observation / Industry Insight

Comment on something you've noticed — in your industry, in business, in the market. These work well for thought leaders and consultants.

Structure:

  • Hook: "I've noticed something about [topic]..."
  • Observation: 2–3 lines expanding on the pattern
  • Why it matters: One implication
  • Your take: Where this is going or what to do about it
  • CTA: "Am I wrong?"

Example hook: "I've noticed that the B2B companies struggling most right now have one thing in common..."

LinkedIn Post Checklist (Use Before Publishing)

Hook test: Read only the first line. Does it create enough curiosity to continue? If not, rewrite it.

Format test: Are there enough line breaks? LinkedIn rewards posts where each line is 1–2 sentences maximum. Long paragraphs kill reach.

Value test: Would someone save this? If the entire value is just your opinion with no actionable insight, it'll underperform.

CTA test: Does the last line invite a response? A question, "Save this," or a direct statement works better than no CTA.

Hashtag check: Use 3–5 relevant hashtags at the bottom (not embedded in the text). More than 5 looks spammy.
