15 ChatGPT Prompts for Lawyers That Save Hours Every Week

Legal work is writing-intensive — client communications, educational content, intake forms, and business development. These prompts cut the blank-page problem for every non-billable writing task so you can focus on client work.

Prompt 1 of 15
Practice area explainer (plain English)
Write a plain-English explainer about [LEGAL TOPIC/PRACTICE AREA] for potential clients who have no legal background. Cover: what it is, when someone needs it, what the process generally looks like, and what questions to ask an attorney. Under 300 words. Avoid jargon. Add a disclaimer: 'This is general information, not legal advice.'
💡 Pro tip: Educational content builds trust before a consultation. Add to your website FAQ or blog.
Prompt 2 of 15
Client intake questionnaire
Create a client intake questionnaire for a [PRACTICE AREA] matter. Include: contact details, nature of the matter (3-4 options), timeline of key events, relevant parties involved, prior legal representation, desired outcome, and urgency level. Format as a numbered list with sub-bullets for follow-up questions. 15-20 questions total.
💡 Pro tip: A well-structured intake saves 30 minutes per new client and improves your case analysis.
Prompt 3 of 15
Case update email (status update to client)
Write a case status update email to client [CLIENT NAME] regarding their [MATTER TYPE] matter. Update them on: [RECENT DEVELOPMENT], what happens next ([NEXT STEP]), and expected timeline ([TIMEFRAME]). Reassure without over-promising. Under 200 words. Tone: professional, warm, clear.
💡 Pro tip: Clients who receive regular updates generate fewer 'checking in' calls and give better reviews.
Prompt 4 of 15
LinkedIn post (thought leadership)
Write a LinkedIn post about a common misconception in [PRACTICE AREA]. Structure: 1-line hook that challenges the misconception, 3-4 short paragraphs explaining the reality with a real example (anonymized), and a takeaway for potential clients. Under 1,200 characters. No legalese. End with a soft CTA to contact for a consultation.
💡 Pro tip: Educational LinkedIn posts from attorneys outperform promotional posts 5:1 in engagement.
Prompt 5 of 15
FAQ blog post (SEO)
Write a 500-word FAQ blog post answering: 'What is [LEGAL QUESTION — e.g., the difference between a will and a trust]?' Structure: Brief intro, 5 specific questions with concise answers (50-75 words each), and a conclusion with CTA to schedule a consultation. Target keyword: '[KEYWORD]'. Include a legal disclaimer at the end.
💡 Pro tip: FAQ pages targeting 'what is' and 'how does' questions rank quickly for informational searches.
Prompt 6 of 15
Referral thank-you note (to referring attorney)
Write a professional thank-you note to attorney [NAME] at [FIRM] for referring [GENERAL MATTER TYPE — no client details]. Express genuine appreciation, mention the outcome (if shareable), and reinforce my commitment to returning the quality. Offer to reciprocate referrals in [MY PRACTICE AREAS]. Under 150 words. Tone: collegial, warm.
💡 Pro tip: Handwritten notes work better than emails for attorney-to-attorney referral relationships.
Prompt 7 of 15
Engagement letter introduction paragraph
Write the introduction paragraph of an engagement letter for a new client retaining our firm for [MATTER TYPE]. Confirm: scope of representation, fee arrangement ([FEE TYPE]), communication expectations, and what is NOT included in the representation. Professional but approachable. Under 150 words. Note: this is a template — actual letters require attorney review.
💡 Pro tip: Clear engagement letters reduce fee disputes and scope creep significantly.
Prompt 8 of 15
Settlement explanation email
Write an email to client [CLIENT NAME] explaining a settlement offer of [AMOUNT] for their [MATTER TYPE] case. Explain: what the offer covers, the pros and cons of accepting vs. proceeding to trial, timeline comparison, and legal fees impact. Conclude with a request to schedule a call to discuss. Under 300 words. Balanced, not biased toward either outcome.
💡 Pro tip: Clients make better decisions when presented with a balanced pros/cons analysis, not just a recommendation.
Prompt 9 of 15
Google review request (post-resolution)
Write an email to a former client [CLIENT NAME] requesting a Google review after successfully resolving their [MATTER TYPE] matter. Express pride in the outcome, explain the value of reviews for helping others find qualified legal help, include a direct link to your Google review page [LINK], and keep the ask low-friction. Under 100 words.
💡 Pro tip: Legal clients rarely leave reviews unprompted — one direct ask doubles your response rate.
Prompt 10 of 15
Demand letter opening paragraph
Write the opening paragraph of a demand letter on behalf of [CLIENT NAME] to [RECIPIENT] regarding [CLAIM TYPE]. State the identity of client and attorney, the nature of the dispute, the applicable legal basis (general — attorney must fill specifics), and the demand being made. Firm, professional tone. Under 150 words. Template only — requires attorney review.
💡 Pro tip: A clear, firm opening paragraph sets the tone and often resolves matters before litigation.
Prompt 11 of 15
Website bio (attorney profile)
Write a professional bio for attorney [NAME] specializing in [PRACTICE AREAS] at [FIRM NAME] in [CITY]. Include: years of experience, key practice areas, notable accomplishments (if any), bar admissions, education, and a one-sentence personal note to humanize the profile. Under 200 words. Third-person. Avoid superlatives like 'best' or 'leading'.
💡 Pro tip: Bios written in third person rank better for '[city] [practice area] attorney' searches.
Prompt 12 of 15
Newsletter intro (monthly client update)
Write the introductory section of a monthly legal newsletter for [PRACTICE AREA] clients. Include: one recent legal development relevant to clients in [MONTH/YEAR], its practical impact in plain English, and a brief note about what the firm is working on. Under 200 words. Tone: informative, not alarmist.
💡 Pro tip: Monthly newsletters keep you top-of-mind when clients need referrals or additional matters.
Prompt 13 of 15
Consultation call script opener
Write the opening 3 minutes of a script for an initial consultation call with a prospective client who inquired about [MATTER TYPE]. Cover: warm welcome, brief agenda for the call, 3 key questions to understand their situation, and a transition to listening. Natural, conversational. Do not pitch services in the first 3 minutes.
💡 Pro tip: The first 3 minutes determine whether a prospect feels heard. Don't rush to solutions.
Prompt 14 of 15
Adverse party negotiation email
Write a negotiation email to opposing counsel [NAME] regarding [MATTER SUMMARY]. Propose: [YOUR CLIENT'S POSITION], acknowledge [OPPOSING PARTY'S CONCERN], and suggest a middle-ground solution of [PROPOSED RESOLUTION]. Tone: professional, solution-oriented, not combative. Under 200 words. Template — attorney must review.
💡 Pro tip: Collaborative framing in early negotiation emails reduces the time to resolution.
Prompt 15 of 15
Service page copy (practice area)
Write website copy for a [PRACTICE AREA] service page. Include: headline targeting the client's fear/need, 3 common situations that bring clients to us, what we do and how we help, why choose us (3 differentiators), and a CTA to schedule a consultation. Under 350 words. No legal jargon. SEO-friendly.
💡 Pro tip: Practice area pages optimized for intent-based queries ('divorce attorney [city]') drive the best consultation requests.
