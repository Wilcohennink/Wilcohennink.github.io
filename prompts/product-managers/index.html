15 ChatGPT Prompts for Product Managers That Cut Your Writing Time in Half

Product managers write constantly — requirements docs, stakeholder updates, user stories, launch notes. These prompts speed up every writing task so you can spend more time talking to users and making product decisions.

Prompt 1 of 15
Product Requirements Document (PRD)
Write a Product Requirements Document for [FEATURE NAME]. Include: problem statement (why this matters), goals and success metrics (3 OKRs/KPIs), user personas affected (2-3), functional requirements (10 user stories in 'As a [user], I want... so that...' format), non-functional requirements (3 — performance, security, accessibility), out-of-scope items, and open questions. Professional, concise.
💡 Pro tip: PRDs that include explicit 'out-of-scope' sections reduce scope creep by 40% during development.
Prompt 2 of 15
User story (with acceptance criteria)
Write a user story and acceptance criteria for [FEATURE/FUNCTIONALITY]. User story format: 'As a [USER TYPE], I want [CAPABILITY] so that [BENEFIT].' Then write 5 acceptance criteria in Given/When/Then format. Include 2 edge cases. Under 200 words total.
💡 Pro tip: Given/When/Then acceptance criteria can be directly converted to test cases by QA — saving both teams time.
Prompt 3 of 15
Product roadmap narrative (quarterly)
Write a product roadmap narrative for [PRODUCT NAME] for [Q/YEAR]. Cover: the strategic themes for this quarter (3, tied to company OKRs), key initiatives per theme (2-3 each), what we're NOT doing this quarter and why (important for alignment), and the key dependencies or risks. For sharing with stakeholders. Under 400 words. Clear, strategic.
💡 Pro tip: Roadmap narratives that explain what you're NOT doing build more stakeholder trust than those that only list plans.
Prompt 4 of 15
Stakeholder update email (weekly/bi-weekly)
Write a stakeholder update email for [PRODUCT/FEATURE] for the week of [DATE]. Include: key progress (3 bullets), blockers or risks (1-2 with owner and resolution plan), metrics update ([KEY METRIC] vs. target), and decisions needed from stakeholders. Under 250 words. Scannable, direct.
💡 Pro tip: Stakeholder emails that lead with what you need from them (not just what you've done) get faster responses.
Prompt 5 of 15
Launch announcement (internal + external)
Write two versions of a launch announcement for [FEATURE/PRODUCT NAME]: (1) Internal (for the company): what we shipped, how it works, metrics we're tracking, known limitations, who to contact with questions. (2) External (for users/customers): the problem it solves, how to use it, link to documentation or onboarding. Each under 250 words.
💡 Pro tip: Separate internal and external launch announcements prevent internal context (known bugs, sales caveats) from reaching customers.
Prompt 6 of 15
User interview discussion guide
Write a discussion guide for a user research interview about [TOPIC/FEATURE AREA]. Duration: 30-45 minutes. Include: intro and consent (2 minutes), warm-up questions (2), main topic questions (5-6 open-ended), follow-up probes for each, and a closing section. Total 12-15 questions. Focus on behavior and past experience, not opinions about hypothetical features.
💡 Pro tip: Ask about past behavior ('tell me about the last time you...') not hypotheticals ('would you use this feature?') — behavior data is far more reliable.
Prompt 7 of 15
A/B test hypothesis
Write a structured A/B test hypothesis for testing [CHANGE — e.g., a new CTA, a redesigned onboarding flow, a different pricing page]. Use the format: 'We believe that [CHANGE] will cause [USER/METRIC] to [OUTCOME] because [RATIONALE]. We'll know this is true if [METRIC] changes by [X%] within [TIMEFRAME].' Then describe the control and variant in 2-3 sentences each.
💡 Pro tip: Documented hypotheses with explicit success metrics prevent post-test rationalization ('the results were inconclusive').
Prompt 8 of 15
Feature prioritization memo
Write a feature prioritization memo for the [PRODUCT/TEAM NAME] backlog going into [QUARTER]. Use the RICE framework (Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort) to evaluate these 5 features: [FEATURE 1], [FEATURE 2], [FEATURE 3], [FEATURE 4], [FEATURE 5]. Score each (1-10 for each factor), calculate RICE score, and recommend the top 2 to build next with a brief justification.
💡 Pro tip: Documented RICE scores make prioritization conversations data-driven rather than political — whoever shouts loudest loses influence.
Prompt 9 of 15
Competitive analysis summary
Write a competitive analysis summary for [PRODUCT NAME] vs. [COMPETITOR 1] and [COMPETITOR 2] in [PRODUCT CATEGORY]. Compare on: core features, pricing, target customer, key differentiators, and known weaknesses. Format as a 3-column table followed by a 150-word strategic summary: where we win, where we lose, and our recommended response.
💡 Pro tip: Competitive analyses that recommend specific product responses (not just list differences) are actionable. The comparison alone isn't enough.
Prompt 10 of 15
Sprint review summary (for stakeholders)
Write a sprint review summary for Sprint [NUMBER] at [COMPANY/TEAM]. Include: sprint goal (achieved/not achieved + why), stories completed (brief summary of each), stories carried over (with reason), demo highlights (2-3 bullets), metrics impact (if any), and sprint [N+1] preview. Under 300 words. For non-technical stakeholder audience.
💡 Pro tip: Sprint reviews written for non-technical stakeholders reduce the number of 'what exactly did you build?' questions.
Prompt 11 of 15
Customer feedback synthesis (qualitative)
I have [X] pieces of user feedback about [FEATURE/PRODUCT AREA]. Here are the key themes I've identified: [PASTE OR DESCRIBE THEMES]. Help me write a structured feedback synthesis document that includes: top 3 pain points (with supporting evidence), top 3 requests (with frequency), conflicting signals (what users disagree on), and recommended next steps. Under 400 words.
💡 Pro tip: Feedback syntheses that include conflicting signals (not just consensus) lead to better product decisions.
Prompt 12 of 15
Onboarding flow improvement proposal
Write a proposal to improve the onboarding flow for [PRODUCT NAME]. Current onboarding: [DESCRIBE CURRENT STATE]. Problem: [DROP-OFF RATE/FRICTION POINT]. Proposed change: [CHANGE]. Expected impact: [EXPECTED METRIC IMPROVEMENT]. Include: before/after flow comparison (3-4 steps), success metric, and rollout approach (A/B test vs. full ship). Under 300 words.
💡 Pro tip: Onboarding improvement proposals with a specific drop-off metric (not just 'users are confused') get prioritized faster.
Prompt 13 of 15
Go-to-market plan (feature launch)
Write a go-to-market plan for launching [FEATURE NAME] to [AUDIENCE — all users / power users / specific segment]. Include: launch date, target segment, messaging (key benefit in 1 sentence), channels (in-app, email, blog, social), success metrics (3), phased rollout approach (if applicable), and post-launch review cadence. Under 400 words.
💡 Pro tip: GTM plans with a phased rollout (% of users by week) reduce risk and let you catch issues before full release.
Prompt 14 of 15
Post-mortem write-up (incident or failed feature)
Write a post-mortem for [INCIDENT/FAILED FEATURE] on [DATE]. Include: summary (what happened), timeline (key events), root cause analysis (5 Whys), impact (users/revenue/metrics affected), what went well, what didn't, and 3 action items with owners and deadlines. Blameless framing — focus on systems, not people. Under 400 words.
💡 Pro tip: Blameless post-mortems that focus on systems (not who made a mistake) generate better action items and healthier team culture.
Prompt 15 of 15
Executive summary (product strategy, board-level)
Write an executive summary of [PRODUCT NAME]'s strategy for [YEAR] for board/executive review. Cover: market opportunity, our current position, 3 strategic bets for the year, key risks and mitigations, and resource requirements. Under 350 words. High-altitude, decisive, no implementation detail.
💡 Pro tip: Executive summaries that name 3 clear bets (and explicitly say what you're not doing) are 5x more useful to boards than comprehensive strategic documents.
