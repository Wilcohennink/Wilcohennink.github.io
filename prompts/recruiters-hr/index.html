15 ChatGPT Prompts for Recruiters & HR That Save Hours Every Week

Recruiting is a writing-heavy job — job descriptions, candidate outreach, interview kits, offer letters. These prompts cut the drafting time so you can spend more time on the work that actually matters: evaluating people.

Prompt 1 of 15
Job description (inclusive, modern)
Write a job description for a [JOB TITLE] at [COMPANY TYPE] in [LOCATION/REMOTE]. Include: brief company intro (2 sentences), role summary, 5 key responsibilities (bullet points), 5 required qualifications, 3 nice-to-haves, compensation range [RANGE], and benefits. Write in second person ('you will'). Remove gendered language. Under 450 words.
💡 Pro tip: Inclusive job descriptions (removing coded language like 'aggressive', 'ninja') increase qualified applications by 40%.
Prompt 2 of 15
LinkedIn recruiter outreach message
Write a LinkedIn InMail to [CANDIDATE NAME], a [TITLE] at [COMPANY] about an opportunity at [MY COMPANY] as a [ROLE]. Lead with a specific reason you reached out to them (their experience in [X]). Describe the role in 1 sentence. Ask if they're open to a 15-minute conversation. Under 150 characters for the note, 300 for the InMail. No copy-paste feel.
💡 Pro tip: Messages that reference specific experience get 4x more replies than generic 'great opportunity' InMails.
Prompt 3 of 15
Interview question kit (behavioral)
Create a behavioral interview question kit for a [JOB TITLE] role. Include 10 questions using the STAR format (Situation, Task, Action, Result). Cover these competencies: [LIST 4-5 — e.g., problem-solving, collaboration, leadership, adaptability]. For each question, add: what you're evaluating and a red flag answer to watch for.
💡 Pro tip: Competency-based question kits reduce interview-to-hire inconsistency across multiple interviewers.
Prompt 4 of 15
Candidate rejection email (post-interview)
Write a respectful rejection email to candidate [NAME] who interviewed for [ROLE]. Thank them for their time, let them down with kindness, avoid specific reasons (for legal safety), and invite them to apply for future roles if appropriate. Under 100 words. Warm, not corporate-cold.
💡 Pro tip: Good rejection emails protect your employer brand. Candidates talk to others in their network.
Prompt 5 of 15
Offer letter (key components)
Write the core body of an employment offer letter for [CANDIDATE NAME] for the role of [JOB TITLE] at [COMPANY]. Include: start date, reporting manager, compensation ([SALARY] + [BONUS/EQUITY if any]), work location, employment type, and offer expiry date ([DATE]). Professional, clear. Note: this is a template requiring legal review before sending.
💡 Pro tip: Clear, warm offer letters reduce the time-to-acceptance window — ambiguity causes second thoughts.
Prompt 6 of 15
Cold sourcing email (passive candidate)
Write a cold sourcing email to [CANDIDATE NAME], a passive candidate who currently works at [COMPANY] as a [TITLE]. Don't lead with the job description. Instead: mention something notable about their background ([EXPERIENCE/ACHIEVEMENT]), describe the opportunity in terms of what it would mean for their career, and ask if they'd be open to a casual conversation. Under 150 words.
💡 Pro tip: Career-growth framing ('a step up in scope') outperforms compensation pitches for passive candidate outreach.
Prompt 7 of 15
Employer brand social post
Write a LinkedIn post showcasing [COMPANY NAME]'s workplace culture. Feature: [ONE SPECIFIC TEAM INITIATIVE, PERK, OR VALUE IN ACTION — e.g., 'our engineering team just shipped X', 'we gave everyone a mental health day']. Make it authentic — not a PR release. Include a subtle hiring CTA. Under 1,200 characters.
💡 Pro tip: Authentic culture posts (real moments, not stock photos) generate 8x more engagement than promotional hiring posts.
Prompt 8 of 15
New hire welcome email (day-1 prep)
Write a welcome email to new hire [NAME] starting as [JOB TITLE] on [START DATE]. Include: welcome from the team, what to expect on Day 1 (location/link, who to ask for, schedule overview), what to bring or prepare, and an expression of excitement about them joining. Under 200 words. Warm, organized.
💡 Pro tip: New hires decide whether to stay long-term within the first 45 days. A warm, organized welcome reduces early attrition.
Prompt 9 of 15
Performance review template (manager)
Write a performance review template for a [JOB TITLE] covering a [QUARTERLY/ANNUAL] review period. Include sections for: overall performance rating (scale explanation), goal achievement (list last period's goals + outcome), key strengths (2-3), areas for development (2-3), goals for next period (3 SMART goals), and manager comments. Professional, growth-focused.
💡 Pro tip: Structured performance reviews reduce manager bias and make conversations more productive.
Prompt 10 of 15
Candidate experience survey (post-process)
Write a 5-question candidate experience survey to send after an interview process (regardless of outcome). Ask about: communication quality, interview experience, process length, overall impression of the company, and likelihood to recommend the company to others (NPS). Professional, brief. Under 5 minutes to complete.
💡 Pro tip: Candidate NPS data is one of the most underused employer brand improvement tools.
Prompt 11 of 15
Internal job posting (internal mobility)
Write an internal job posting for [ROLE] to share with existing employees. Frame it as a growth opportunity. Include: what the role involves, what profile you're looking for (skills, not seniority), how to apply internally, and the application deadline. Encourage anyone interested to have a conversation first. Under 250 words. Inclusive, encouraging.
💡 Pro tip: Internal mobility reduces attrition — employees who see promotion paths stay 40% longer.
Prompt 12 of 15
Recruiter cold call script (30 seconds)
Write a 30-second cold call opening script for reaching a passive candidate [CANDIDATE NAME] by phone. Purpose: get them to agree to a 10-minute follow-up call. Include: who I am, why I'm calling (specific relevance to their background), and a low-friction ask. Natural, not robotic. Two versions: one for voicemail, one for a live pick-up.
💡 Pro tip: If they pick up, you have 8 seconds to create relevance before they mentally check out.
Prompt 13 of 15
Decline a recruiter (template for candidates)
Write a polite email template for a candidate to decline a recruiter's outreach. Keep it short, appreciative, and open for future opportunities. Under 75 words. Professional, no excuses needed.
💡 Pro tip: Good recruiter relationships are long-term. A professional decline now can be a hire 2 years later.
Prompt 14 of 15
Hiring manager kick-off meeting agenda
Write an agenda for a recruiter-hiring manager kick-off meeting for a new [JOB TITLE] search. Cover: role overview and must-haves, what 'great' looks like vs. 'good enough', compensation and competing offers in the market, interview process and ownership, timelines, and success metrics. 45-minute meeting. Include preparation questions for the hiring manager.
💡 Pro tip: Kick-off meetings that establish 'great vs. good enough' early cut interview cycles by 30%.
Prompt 15 of 15
Team announcement (new hire)
Write a team announcement email for new hire [NAME] joining as [JOB TITLE] on [DATE]. Include: their background, what they'll be working on, a personal detail they've shared (favorite team thing, hobby), and how to welcome them. Under 150 words. Energetic, human.
💡 Pro tip: Personal details in new hire announcements reduce the social awkwardness of week 1 significantly.
