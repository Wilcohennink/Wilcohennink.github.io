15 ChatGPT Prompts for Architects That Win Better Projects

Architecture firms win on reputation, relationships, and the ability to communicate complex ideas simply. These prompts help you write the proposals, portfolio narratives, and content that attract the projects you actually want.

Prompt 1 of 15
Project proposal (design competition)
Write a project proposal narrative for [PROJECT NAME], a [PROJECT TYPE] design for [CLIENT/BRIEF]. Include: our design concept (key idea in 2-3 sentences), how the concept responds to [SITE/BRIEF/CONTEXT], 3 key design moves and their rationale, sustainability approach, and why our firm is positioned to deliver this project. Under 400 words. Conceptually confident, clear.
💡 Pro tip: Architecture proposals that articulate one clear design concept (not three competing ideas) are selected more often by review panels.
Prompt 2 of 15
Project description (portfolio/website)
Write a portfolio project description for [PROJECT NAME] on [FIRM NAME]'s website. Include: project type and location, the design challenge or brief, the core concept, key design decisions and their rationale, materials and construction approach, and the project's outcome or impact. 200-250 words. Evocative but precise.
💡 Pro tip: Portfolio descriptions that explain the 'why' behind design decisions attract clients who value design thinking — your best client type.
Prompt 3 of 15
Award submission narrative
Write an award submission narrative for [PROJECT NAME] for the [AWARD NAME] in the [CATEGORY] category. Cover: project summary (brief + site + client), the design problem and opportunity, design concept and process, technical innovation or sustainability achievements, and community/client impact. Under 500 words. Compelling, comprehensive.
💡 Pro tip: Award submissions that tie design decisions to measurable outcomes (energy use reduction, community engagement metrics) are rated highest by technical jury panels.
Prompt 4 of 15
Client update email (design development stage)
Write a design development update email to client [NAME] for [PROJECT NAME]. Cover: what was completed this stage, key design decisions made (2-3), any issues resolved or remaining, what happens in the next stage, and any decisions or approvals needed from the client. Under 250 words. Clear, organized, professional.
💡 Pro tip: Proactive update emails that include decisions needed from the client prevent delays and make clients feel in control.
Prompt 5 of 15
LinkedIn post (thought leadership)
Write a LinkedIn post about [ARCHITECTURAL TOPIC — urban design trend, sustainability approach, adaptive reuse, etc.]. Audience: developers, planners, fellow architects, potential clients. Structure: 1-line insight hook, 2-3 paragraphs with a specific project or example to ground the argument, and a final takeaway or question. Under 1,200 characters. Opinionated, not academic.
💡 Pro tip: Architects who share specific project learnings (not general principles) build stronger LinkedIn followings in their target client demographics.
Prompt 6 of 15
Firm introduction email (cold to developer)
Write a cold introduction email from [FIRM NAME] to [DEVELOPER/CORPORATION] who is developing [PROJECT TYPE] in [CITY]. Lead with a relevant completed project that demonstrates alignment with their development goals. Propose a brief introductory meeting. Under 150 words. Project-led, not credential-led.
💡 Pro tip: Architecture firm cold emails that lead with a relevant completed project get 4x more replies than those that lead with credentials.
Prompt 7 of 15
Sustainability statement (ESG / design philosophy)
Write a sustainability design statement for [FIRM NAME] for use on the website, award submissions, and RFQ responses. Cover: the firm's core sustainability philosophy, 3 specific approaches used across projects (passive design, materials, lifecycle thinking), and a recent project example. Under 250 words. Substantive, not greenwashing.
💡 Pro tip: Sustainability statements that cite specific approaches and real project outcomes are credible. Vague 'we care about the environment' language is ignored.
Prompt 8 of 15
RFQ / RFP response cover letter
Write a cover letter for [FIRM NAME]'s response to an RFQ/RFP for [PROJECT TYPE] issued by [CLIENT/ORGANIZATION]. Cover: our interest and fit for this project (2-3 reasons), a relevant completed project (brief), our proposed approach to this specific brief, and an invitation to meet. Under 250 words. Tailored to the specific RFQ, confident.
💡 Pro tip: RFQ cover letters that reference specific elements of the brief (not just the project type) score significantly higher on initial shortlisting.
Prompt 9 of 15
Project close-out summary (to client)
Write a project close-out summary email to client [NAME] for [PROJECT NAME]. Cover: brief summary of what was delivered, key milestones and any notable achievements, how to contact us for post-occupancy issues, warranty and maintenance information ([SYSTEMS/ELEMENTS]), and an invitation to stay in touch. Under 250 words. Closing a partnership well.
💡 Pro tip: Project close-out communications that set up clear ongoing support channels generate the most repeat business and referrals.
Prompt 10 of 15
Social media post (site visit / construction progress)
Write 3 social media post variations for a [CONSTRUCTION PROGRESS / SITE VISIT / DESIGN MILESTONE] at [PROJECT NAME]. Platform: [Instagram/LinkedIn]. Include: what stage the project is at, one interesting design or construction detail, and a visual description prompt for the photo. 3-5 hashtags. Each under 200 words.
💡 Pro tip: Construction progress posts humanize the architectural process and create ongoing engagement with clients, prospective clients, and future collaborators.
Prompt 11 of 15
Firm capabilities statement (for submissions)
Write a firm capabilities statement for [FIRM NAME] for use in RFQ responses and award submissions. Cover: firm overview (size, location, founding year), primary project types and sectors, selected project highlights (3 brief descriptions), key team members and their roles, and design philosophy (2-3 sentences). Under 350 words. Factual, differentiated.
💡 Pro tip: Capabilities statements that lead with the most relevant project type for each specific submission perform significantly better than generic firm brochures.
Prompt 12 of 15
Client testimonial request email
Write an email to client [NAME] requesting a testimonial for [FIRM NAME] at the end of [PROJECT NAME]. Offer 3 reflection questions: (1) What was your challenge or goal when you came to us? (2) What was your experience working with the firm? (3) How has the completed project met or exceeded your expectations? Under 100 words. Low-friction, specific.
💡 Pro tip: Architecture client testimonials that describe the collaboration process (not just the finished building) attract more new clients — future clients are buying the relationship.
Prompt 13 of 15
Instagram architecture content strategy
Build a 3-month Instagram content strategy for [FIRM NAME] targeting [AUDIENCE — developers, homeowners, design community]. Suggest: 4 content pillars with 3 post ideas each, ideal posting frequency, hashtag strategy (3 sets of 5-10 hashtags by category), and the one type of content that performs best in the architecture niche. Month-by-month focus themes.
💡 Pro tip: Architecture firms on Instagram that commit to one content pillar consistently (construction progress, design details, or project stories) grow faster than those posting inconsistently across all types.
Prompt 14 of 15
Press release (project completion / award win)
Write a press release for [FIRM NAME] announcing the [COMPLETION/AWARD WIN] of [PROJECT NAME]. Include: headline, dateline, lead paragraph (who, what, where, when), design description (2-3 sentences, client-accessible language), principal architect quote, notable technical achievement, and boilerplate 'About [Firm Name]' section. Standard AP style. Under 400 words.
💡 Pro tip: Architecture press releases with a compelling principal quote and a specific technical achievement (first certified Passivhaus in [city]) get picked up most often.
Prompt 15 of 15
Website bio (principal architect)
Write a website bio for principal architect [NAME] at [FIRM NAME]. Include: educational background, career focus and design approach, notable projects (2-3 named), awards or recognition (if any), professional memberships, and one personal line that reveals their architectural values. Under 250 words. Third person. Confident, human, specific.
💡 Pro tip: Principal bios that reveal a specific design philosophy ('I believe every project should strengthen the street it's on') attract clients who share that value.
